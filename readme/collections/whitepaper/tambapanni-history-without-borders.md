## I. Methodological Preamble

Every history is written through a lens. The question is whether the historian acknowledges the lens. The dominant historiography of the island known today as Sri Lanka — in antiquity Tambapanni to its earliest inhabitants, Taprobane to Greek and Roman traders, Serendib to Arab navigators — has been written through three overlapping lenses that systematically distort the evidence: the post-Westphalian nation-state framework, the Germanic Aryan philological tradition, and the colonial administrative need for fixed ethnic categories.

\
<br>

This document does not claim to be free of perspective. It makes its perspective explicit, and follows two foundational principles: that the political problems of the island cannot be resolved without understanding the false historical foundation on which they have been built; and that understanding that foundation requires a framework for what a modern democratic state actually requires — and why the island has never had one.

\
<br>

### Why a Racial or Religious Ethos Cannot Found a Modern Democratic State

A modern democratic state rests on three institutional pillars: an effective and impartial state apparatus; the rule of law that binds even the most powerful actors; and democratic accountability through which citizens hold the state to account.<sup>\[5,25]</sup>

\
<br>

The critical word is impartial. A state apparatus is impartial when it serves all citizens equally regardless of ethnicity, religion, language, or lineage — when its bureaucrats, judges, police, and military are recruited on merit and bound by rules rather than by loyalty to a particular community. This is what distinguishes a modern state from a patronage network.<sup>\[25]</sup>

\
<br>

A state founded on a racial or religious ethos — on the principle that one ethnic or religious group is the primary or rightful custodian of the state — structurally cannot achieve this impartiality. The founding principle itself designates who the state serves. Every institution built on that principle carries the designation forward. Minority communities are then not citizens of a shared state but subjects of someone else's state — at best tolerated, at worst excluded.<sup>\[25]</sup>

\
<br>

This is not an abstract argument. It is the precise mechanism by which the Sinhalese Buddhist nationalist project, once captured into state power through democratic elections, converted democratic institutions into ethnic instruments. The Sinhala Only Act of 1956 was not a policy disagreement. It was the state's definition of who belonged to it.

\
<br>

The deeper problem is that racial and religious ethos in state-founding principles is incompatible with the rule of law itself. The rule of law requires that law bind all equally — including the state. A state whose founding principle designates a preferred community cannot be genuinely bound by law when law conflicts with that designation. The designation will always override the law, through legislation if possible, through communal pressure if necessary.<sup>\[5]</sup>

\
<br>

### Sri Lanka Democratized Before It Built a Strong, Impartial State

Fukuyama's analysis of political sequencing establishes that the order in which state institutions develop has profound consequences for their eventual character.<sup>\[25]</sup>

\
<br>

The correct developmental sequence is: first, a strong and impartial state apparatus; second, the rule of law constraining that apparatus; third, democratic accountability enabling citizens to direct the apparatus. Countries that followed this sequence — Prussia, Japan, South Korea — developed bureaucracies with their own professional character, resistant to capture by particular factions.<sup>\[25]</sup>

\
<br>

Countries that democratized before building a strong impartial state faced a different outcome. When popular elections arrive before a professional, meritocratic, rule-following bureaucracy exists, the state apparatus becomes a prize of democratic competition. Politicians winning elections distribute state employment, contracts, and resources to their supporters. The state is not impartial — it is clientelist. It serves whoever wins the election, not the citizenry as a whole.<sup>\[25]</sup>

\
<br>

_When popular democratization precedes the development of a strong, autonomous administrative state, the executive branch is captured as a prize of clientelism._<sup>\[25]</sup>

\
<br>

This is precisely the trajectory of Sri Lanka. Universal suffrage was introduced in 1931 under the Donoughmore Constitution — before independence, before a professional state apparatus existed, before the rule of law had been established as genuinely binding on political actors.<sup>\[26,27]</sup>

\
<br>

The result was that democratic competition immediately became a competition for ethnic patronage. Political parties competed by offering ethnic constituencies what they wanted — not because ethnic division was the underlying reality, but because ethnic identity was the available mobilising vehicle in the absence of a civic, impartial state framework to compete within.<sup>\[27]</sup>

\
<br>

The Sinhala Only Act of 1956, the communal riots of 1958, the constitutional removal of minority protections in 1972, the systematic marginalisation of Tamil civil servants and university entrants — these were not failures of democracy. They were the logical outputs of a democratic system operating without an impartial state to constrain what democracy could do. Majorities could use democratic institutions to serve majority interests because there was no strong impartial state standing between democratic outcomes and their implementation.<sup>\[27]</sup>

\
<br>

The consequence is the central problem this document identifies: the problems of Tambapanni will never be resolved if they continue to be approached as a conflict between two ancient communities with irreconcilable identities. They are a structural problem of state formation — a state that was handed democratic machinery before it had been built as an impartial institution, and that was then captured by an ethnic nationalist project that made impartiality structurally impossible.

\
<br>

The historical analysis that follows is therefore not purely academic. Understanding that the two communities were not ancient enemies — that they were one civilisation whose division was manufactured — is the necessary precondition for understanding what the state of Tambapanni would need to become in order to function as a genuine modern democracy: impartial, civic, and prior to ethnicity in its institutional logic.<sup>\[5,25]</sup>

\
<br>

### The Lens Problem

Political and social organisation in pre-modern societies operated on fundamentally different logics than those we assume today.<sup>\[5]</sup>

\
<br>

Sovereignty was personal and dynastic, not territorial. Boundaries were fluid, seasonal, and negotiated, not fixed and defended. Loyalty was to a person, a lineage, or a ritual tradition — not to a geographic unit we would recognise as a nation-state. When we say 'South Indian invasion of Tambapanni,' we commit a category error as profound as saying 'Vijaya invaded Tambapanni.' If one is a migration or a founding, so is the other. The asymmetry in how we apply the word invasion is not a historical judgement — it is a political one, imported from the 20th century.

\

## II. Sources and Their Limitations

Before any analysis of the Vijaya origin question or the Mahavamsa's institutional context can proceed, the nature and limitations of the available sources must be established. Readers unfamiliar with the source criticism summarised here should consult it before proceeding, as it qualifies every claim made in subsequent sections.

\
<br>

### The Translation Chain

The primary source for the Vijaya origin narrative is the Mahavamsa, a chronicle compiled in Pali by the monk Mahanama in the 5th or 6th century AD. No undisputed autograph manuscript of the Mahavamsa exists. The text is known through manuscript traditions copied by Sri Lankan Buddhist monasteries over many centuries.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

The first English translation was produced by George Turnour in 1837, published as The Mahawanso by Cotta Church Mission Press, Ceylon.<sup>\[1]</sup>

\
<br>

Critically, contemporary accounts record that Turnour knew no Pali when he began his work. He relied on Buddhist monks as intermediaries over a ten-year research period. These intermediaries brought their own institutional perspectives — almost certainly Mahavihara perspectives — to the translation process. Turnour's 1837 text therefore carries not only his own colonial geographic imagination but the interpretive choices of his monastic informants.<sup>\[1]</sup>

\
<br>

The Government of Ceylon subsequently commissioned Wilhelm Geiger to produce a revised translation. Geiger's 1912 edition, published by the Pali Text Society, is the standard scholarly reference.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

Geiger's preface explicitly describes his work as a correction of Turnour. However, Geiger operated within the Germanic Aryan philological framework of his era — a tradition that classified languages and peoples in racial hierarchy terms. His interpretive framework was not neutral, and his footnotes carry assumptions that must be read critically rather than accepted as authoritative.<sup>\[4]</sup>

\
<br>

The full mediation chain is therefore: original Pali manuscripts (not directly accessible to most researchers) → Geiger's 1912 corrected translation → modern historiography. Turnour's 1837 translation fed the earlier popular and nationalist historiography. At no point in this chain is unmediated access to the original Pali established for non-specialist readers.

\
<br>

### The Dipavamsa

The Dipavamsa is an earlier Pali chronicle, predating the Mahavamsa. Hermann Oldenberg produced its critical scholarly edition in 1879.<sup>\[3]</sup>

\
<br>

The Dipavamsa is in overall character less supernaturally elaborated than the Mahavamsa. It omits Kuveni and other yaksha supernatural elements that the Mahavamsa includes. Both texts use the word sīha — lion — in describing the ancestor of Vijaya. The claim sometimes found in secondary literature that the Dipavamsa describes a human bandit named Sinha rather than a lion is a modern scholarly interpretive tradition based on the meaning of the word Sinha, not an explicit textual difference present in Oldenberg's Pali edition. Secondary sources must be distinguished from primary text on this point.<sup>\[3]</sup>

\
<br>

### The Mahavamsa as Institutional Document

The Mahavamsa was authored by Mahavihara monks and reflects Mahavihara institutional interests. It was written in Pali — a liturgical language inaccessible to ordinary people in 5th century Anuradhapura. It was never intended as a popular history. It was institutional correspondence within a learned priestly and royal elite.<sup>\[2,20]</sup>

\
<br>

No independent contemporary external source — not Fa-xian's account of around 400 AD, not any epigraphy, not any Chinese, Roman or Puranic record — corroborates the Vijaya founding narrative as historical event. Fa-xian describes both Mahavihara and Abhayagiri as flourishing institutions without characterising either as orthodox or deviant.<sup>\[6]</sup>

\
<br>

The Mahavamsa's claim to Theravada orthodoxy is self-designation. It exists primarily in documents Mahavihara itself produced. The archaeological evidence shows Abhayagiri was larger than Mahavihara at several points — which materially contradicts the narrative of marginal heterodoxy.<sup>\[20]</sup>

\
<br>

\

## IV. The Sanskrit Indian Ocean World

Before examining the Vijaya origin question, the civilisational context within which the naming conventions of that narrative operated must be established. The founding mythology of Tambapanni does not belong to a single event or a single culture. It belongs to a 1800-year tradition of Sanskrit political naming that radiated across the entire Indian Ocean world — and understanding that tradition is the necessary frame for reading any specific instance within it.

\
<br>

### The Śrī Prefix

Sanskrit Śrī — meaning auspicious, radiant, prosperous — functioned as a consecrating honorific across the Indian Ocean political world.<sup>\[16]</sup>

\
<br>

It appears as a title applied to the Buddha (Śrī Siddhartha), to Vishnu (Śrī Hari), to sacred texts, and to kingdoms. Applying Śrī to a kingdom's name was a deliberate act of political consecration within the Sanskrit cosmological framework — not a geographic description. Sri Lanka means Auspicious Lanka. Srivijaya means Auspicious Victory. The prefix marks kingdoms as participating in the Sanskrit sacred-political order.

\
<br>

### The Lion-City Convention: Siṃha + Pura

Sanskrit Siṃha (lion) + pura (city) produces the lion-city compound deployed across the Indian Ocean world:<sup>\[16,17]</sup>

\
<br>

Sinhapura — the Lala country, western India. Vijaya's birthplace per the Mahavamsa. The founding event and the founding city share the lion etymology — Sihabahu (lion-armed), Sihasivali, Sinhapura, Sinhala.

\
<br>

Singapura — modern Singapore, founded 1299 AD by Sang Nila Utama, a prince of the Srivijayan empire.<sup>\[18]</sup>

\
<br>

The gap between these two deployments of the identical Sanskrit compound in the same Indian Ocean cultural sphere is approximately 1842 years. Lions were never native to the Malay Peninsula or Singapore island. The animal Sang Nila Utama reportedly saw was almost certainly a Malayan tiger.<sup>\[18]</sup>

\
<br>

The lion identification was a conscious deployment of Sanskrit royal legitimacy vocabulary. Sang Nila Utama was not independently discovering the lion-city concept — he was a Srivijayan prince for whom this Sanskrit political toolkit was civilisational inheritance.

\
<br>

### Srivijaya: The Transmission Vehicle

Srivijaya was the maritime empire centred in Sumatra that dominated Indian Ocean trade routes from the 7th to the 13th centuries AD.<sup>\[17]</sup>

\
<br>

Its ruling elite was deeply Indianised — Sanskrit was the court language, both Hindu and Buddhist traditions were patronised, and Sanskrit political vocabulary permeated every dimension of its administrative apparatus. The Srivijayan empire controlled the Strait of Malacca and the trade routes connecting India, Southeast Asia, and China. It was the principal carrier of Indianised political culture into the Malay Archipelago.<sup>\[16,17]</sup>

\
<br>

When Sang Nila Utama named his settlement Singapura, he was deploying naming conventions that 700 years of Srivijayan Sanskrit political culture had made entirely natural. The name Sri Lanka, the name Srivijaya, the name Singapura, the name Sinhapura — these are all products of the same Sanskrit naming culture expressing political legitimacy across the Indian Ocean world.<sup>\[16,18]</sup>

\
<br>

This timeline demonstrates that Sinhapura and Singapura are not two isolated events with a striking etymological coincidence. They are two surviving, named instances within a continuous Sanskrit Indian Ocean political culture — a culture that produced Sri Lanka, Srivijaya, Singapura, and dozens of other Śrī-prefixed and Siṃha-rooted place names across a connected world spanning two millennia and ten thousand kilometres of coastline.

## V. Vijaya and the Origin Question

### The Bengal Misreading: How It Arose

The most widely circulated popular account of Vijaya's origins places him in Bengal — specifically the ancient kingdom of Vanga, corresponding roughly to modern West Bengal and Bangladesh. This reading was not derived from careful reading of the Mahavamsa. It was produced by 19th century colonial-era scholars, principally working through Turnour's 1837 English translation, who encountered the name Vanga in the text and mapped it onto the Bengal familiar to British administrative geography.<sup>\[1]</sup>

\
<br>

Turnour knew no Pali when he began his work. He relied on Mahavihara monks as intermediaries. The Bengal reading he produced was therefore shaped both by his colonial geographic imagination and by the interpretive choices of his informants.<sup>\[1]</sup>

\
<br>

The Bengal origin reading was subsequently absorbed into Sinhalese nationalist historiography because it served a specific ideological purpose. Gujarat, while Indo-Aryan speaking, sits geographically adjacent to the Dravidian zone and had centuries of documented maritime and trading contact with Tamil communities — making it an uncomfortable homeland for a movement insisting on separation from Dravidian identity.<sup>\[12]</sup>

\
<br>

Bengal, by contrast, placed Vijaya at maximum cultural distance from the Dravidian world within the 19th century racial-linguistic imagination the Dharmapala movement had imported from Europe. The Bengal reading was politically convenient first and textually examined second.<sup>\[12]</sup>

\
<br>

It must also be noted that the Indo-Aryan linguistic and cultural origin zone is the Indus-Saraswati basin — modern Punjab, Sindh, Haryana, and the northwest. That is the Vedic heartland. The Rigveda is geographically rooted in the Punjab and Sindh. Bengal was one of the later and more peripheral regions to be absorbed into the Vedic cultural sphere. Gujarat and Sindh are therefore geographically closer to the actual Indo-Aryan origin zone than Bengal is.<sup>\[22]</sup>

\
<br>

### What Geiger's Translation of Chapter 6 Actually Says

A direct reading of Chapter 6 in Geiger's 1912 translation reveals a genealogy considerably more complex than the Bengal origin theory presents.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

The chapter opens not with a Vanga princess but with the daughter of a Kalinga king, who was the consort of the Vanga king. It is this Kalinga-born queen's daughter — of Kalinga-Vanga mixed parentage — who encountered the lion. The encounter did not happen in Vanga. It happened in the Lala country, which the text specifies as where the lion attacked the caravan she had joined. The founding event is geographically located in Lala, not Bengal.

\
<br>

_"In the Lala country a lion attacked the caravan in the forest, the other folk fled this way and that, but she fled along the way by which the lion had come."_<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

From that union in Lala, Sihabahu was born. Sihabahu built Sihapura in the kingdom of Lala and ruled there. Vijaya was born in Sihapura. The text repeats the Lala designation in Chapter 7, in the Buddha's own dying words within the narrative:<sup>\[2,24]</sup>

\
<br>

_"Vijaya, son of king Sihabahu, is come to Lanka from the country of Lala, together with seven hundred followers."_<sup>\[24]</sup>

\
<br>

Vanga is therefore not even Vijaya's grandmother's homeland with precision — it is one half of his great-grandmother's marital context, two full generations and one mythological event removed, and the text explicitly places the founding event geographically in Lala, not Bengal.

\
<br>

### Identifying Lala

The Mahavamsa names Lala as the founding territory but does not itself locate Lala geographically. The identification of Lala with the Gujarat-Sindh coastal region is inference — drawn by combining the text's naming with external sources that independently establish where Lala was as a geographic designation. The inferential character of this identification must be stated clearly.<sup>\[9,10]</sup>

\
<br>

External sources bearing on the location of Lala are as follows. Puranic geographic literature — the Mahabharata and the Vishnu Purana — lists Lata among the western coastal peoples of India, grouping it with regions associated with Gujarat and Sindh. It does not appear in Puranic enumerations of eastern or Bengali peoples.<sup>\[19]</sup>

\
<br>

Xuanzang, travelling in the 7th century AD, placed a region he identified as Lata around modern Bharuch in southern Gujarat — an external geographic witness reflecting a name stable enough to have persisted for centuries.<sup>\[4]</sup>

\
<br>

Ptolemy's 2nd century Geography names a region Larike on the western Indian coast in the same zone, drawing on Roman maritime trading knowledge predating the Mahavamsa entirely.<sup>\[7]</sup>

\
<br>

The Rashtrakuta and Chalukya dynasties used Lata as a formal administrative designation for southern Gujarat across the 7th to 10th centuries — confirming geographic fixity, not mythological vagueness.<sup>\[9]</sup>

\
<br>

None of these sources mention Vijaya or Sinhapura directly. What they collectively establish is that a place called Lala or Lata existed on the western coast of India in the Gujarat region, that this identification is stable across multiple independent traditions spanning several centuries, and that no equivalent evidence places a region called Lala or Lata in Bengal.<sup>\[9,10]</sup>

\
<br>

Professor Barua has objected that a Gujarat origin requires demonstrable dialectical similarity between Gujarati and Sinhala that has not been established.<sup>\[10]</sup>

\
<br>

Professor Chatterjee accepted the Gujarat identification on philological grounds.<sup>\[9]</sup>

\
<br>

The question remains contested within South Asian scholarship. The document presents the Gujarat identification as the better-supported position on the available convergent evidence — not as established consensus.

\
<br>

### The Geography of the Landing

Before reaching Tambapanni, Vijaya first landed at Suppāraka on the western coast of India. Geiger's footnote identifies Suppāraka as Surparaka — modern Sopara in the Thana district, north of Bombay.<sup>\[2,23]</sup>

\
<br>

Sopara's identity as ancient Suppāraka is independently corroborated by an Ashokan edict found at the site and by its appearance in multiple ancient maritime trading records as a major western Indian port.<sup>\[23]</sup>

\
<br>

Vijaya departed from Suppāraka because his followers' violence caused danger there, and only then continued to Tambapanni on the northwest coast of Taprobane. Tambapanni is at Mannar, where the Palk Strait is approximately 30 kilometres wide and connects directly to the Tamil Nadu coast opposite Rameswaram — the natural crossing point for anyone travelling down the western coast of India.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

A departure from Bengal would require either an overland journey across the entire subcontinent or a maritime route around the southern tip of India. A Bengali maritime departure following natural monsoon routes and coastal geography would arrive on the eastern coast of the island — Trincomalee or Batticaloa, not Mannar. The landing site is the unguarded evidence in the text.

\
<br>

### The Shramana Connection

Both Buddhism and Jainism emerge from the Shramana tradition — a non-Vedic philosophical stream whose geographic and cultural heartland was the Indus-Gangetic transition zone, western and central India.<sup>\[21]</sup>

\
<br>

The Shramana tradition predates both Buddhism and Jainism as a recognisable philosophical stream. Its core commitments — ahimsa, rejection of Vedic ritual authority and Brahminical hierarchy, emphasis on individual renunciation, detailed monastic codes — are shared by both traditions in ways that cannot be explained by parallel development.<sup>\[21]</sup>

\
<br>

Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara of Jainism and a contemporary of the Buddha, operated within this same milieu. The historical concentration of Jain communities in Gujarat, Rajasthan, and the western Deccan reflects the western Indian rootedness of the Shramana philosophical tradition.<sup>\[21]</sup>

\
<br>

Early Buddhist and Jain sculptural traditions share deep visual conventions: the seated meditative posture, the ushnisha cranial protrusion, specific hand mudras, the serene facial typology. These shared conventions are most pronounced in western Indian artistic traditions — the early schools associated with Gujarat, Mathura, and the western Deccan.<sup>\[21]</sup>

\
<br>

If Lala was in the Gujarat-Sindh coastal zone, Vijaya's people would have departed from a region where the Shramana philosophical tradition was already deeply embedded. This provides an independent explanatory context for why Buddhism found such rapid and deep adoption in Tambapanni following the founding period. This argument is circumstantial and inferential — a fourth converging dimension, not a proof.<sup>\[21]</sup>

\
<br>

### The Exile Narrative

The Mahavamsa states that Vijaya was exiled by his father Sinhabahu following disruptive behaviour, and that he arrived in Tambapanni as a consequence of that exile. This narrative cannot be independently verified. It exists only in the Mahavamsa and the Dipavamsa — both Mahavihara products. There is no Chinese, Roman, Puranic, or archaeological corroboration.<sup>\[2,3]</sup>

\
<br>

The exile narrative performs several simultaneous functions within the text: it explains the small group (a prince arrived with 700 followers rather than an army); it legitimises the founding as a fresh beginning rather than conquest; and it provides a moral purification arc from disruptive prince to righteous founder.<sup>\[11]</sup>

\
<br>

The 700 followers figure is suspicious as historical record. Round and symbolically resonant numbers in founding narratives are almost universally literary conventions, not census counts.<sup>\[11]</sup>

\
<br>

The structural template is not unique to Tambapanni. Aeneas founds Rome after exile from Troy. Moses leads people out of Egypt to a promised land. Romulus and Remus are abandoned before founding. Exile preceding founding is one of the most universal mythological legitimacy patterns across cultures and centuries. Its presence in the Mahavamsa indicates the authors were working within a recognisable narrative tradition of legitimacy construction.<sup>\[11]</sup>

\
<br>

## VI. The Mahavamsa: Institutional Document, Not Ethnic Manifesto

### The Real Competition

The 5th century context in Anuradhapura was not a conflict between Sinhalese and Tamil peoples. It was a conflict between two Buddhist monastic institutions: Mahavihara and Abhayagiri.<sup>\[20]</sup>

\
<br>

Abhayagiri had for centuries received patronage from kings with Tamil associations, hosted diverse Buddhist traditions including Mahayana influences from South India and beyond, and represented a cosmopolitan vision of Buddhism that threatened Mahavihara's claim to exclusive custodianship of the Theravada tradition. That claim rests almost entirely on Mahavihara's own documents — a circularity the Mahavamsa has never escaped.<sup>\[20]</sup>

\
<br>

The Mahavamsa tied royal authority to Mahavihara validation by constructing a sacred genealogy linking the Sinhalese kings to the Buddha's own ethnic world. This was not ethnic nationalism — it was institutional monopoly strategy.

\
<br>

### The Pali Choice

Mahanama wrote in Pali — a liturgical language that no ordinary person in 5th century Anuradhapura spoke. This single fact dissolves any comparison between the Mahavamsa and Alfred's Anglo-Saxon Chronicle. Alfred wrote in Old English to reach common people and build popular identity. Mahanama wrote in the language of scholars and priests, for scholars, priests, and kings. The Mahavamsa was never intended as popular identity formation.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

### The Elara Paradox

The Mahavamsa's treatment of King Elara — described as a just and noble ruler even as his removal is presented as necessary — reveals the text's own internal tension. The authors could not entirely erase the reality of a shared world in which a Tamil king's justice was acknowledged by Sinhalese subjects. The ethnic boundary the text constructs is visibly artificial even within its own pages.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

\
<br>

## VII. The Colonial Transformation: How a Monastic Document Became an Ethnic Weapon

### The Translation Chain

The Mahavamsa remained inaccessible outside monastic scholarship until the 19th century. Its transformation into an ethnic weapon required specific colonial interventions.<sup>\[1,2]</sup>

\
<br>

1837 — George Turnour, British civil servant, publishes the first English translation of the Mahavamsa. An academic project by a colonial administrator becomes the foundational act of modern Sinhalese ethnic historiography.<sup>\[1]</sup>

\
<br>

1862 onwards — British colonial printing presses in Colombo make Sinhala-language publication possible at scale. The infrastructure for mass dissemination of a previously elite text was built by the coloniser.<sup>\[15]</sup>

\
<br>

1877 onwards — Wilhelm Geiger applies Germanic Aryan philological frameworks to Sinhala. His framework carried racial hierarchy implications within the European academy of his time.<sup>\[4]</sup>

\
<br>

1881 — Pali Text Society founded in London, institutionalising the Mahavamsa as serious historical document.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

1908–1912 — Geiger's German and English translations published.<sup>\[2]</sup>

\
<br>

### The Olcott Intervention

Colonel Henry Olcott arrived in Ceylon in 1880. He was a Freemason before becoming a Theosophist. Theosophy drew from the same Anglo-Protestant millennialist intellectual ecosystem that produced Christian Zionism. Both movements shared racial hierarchy frameworks, chosen people narratives transplanted onto new ethnic groups, and Western esoteric networks as transmission vehicles.<sup>\[13]</sup>

\
<br>

Olcott's Buddhist revival was not a recovery of indigenous tradition. It was the importation of Western racial-spiritual ideology into Buddhist form. The Buddhism he promoted was structured around categories — racial purity, civilisational hierarchy, chosen custodianship — that had no precedent in Theravada tradition but strong precedent in the Protestant and esoteric milieu from which he came.<sup>\[13]</sup>

\
<br>

### Anagarika Dharmapala's Synthesis

Dharmapala, who explicitly described Sinhalese as a pure Aryan race in 1897, completed the synthesis.<sup>\[12]</sup>

\
<br>

He fused Turnour's translation, Geiger's philology, and Olcott's racial-spiritual framework with the Mahavamsa's dynastic genealogy to produce something that had never existed before: Sinhalese Buddhist ethnic nationalism.<sup>\[12,13]</sup>

\
<br>

This construction was entirely exogenous in its intellectual materials. It was assembled from Western racial theory and handed back to Sinhalese political actors as ancient truth. The ideology used to assert the island's pure indigenous identity against Tamil presence and British rule was built entirely from British and German intellectual imports.<sup>\[12]</sup>

\
<br>

\
<br>

## VIII. Tamil Community: A Different Trajectory

Arumuga Navalar's work beginning in 1843 was structurally different from the Sinhalese Buddhist revival.<sup>\[14]</sup>

\
<br>

Navalar's project was the preservation of Saiva literary and ritual tradition against Christian missionary dismantling. It carried no racial purity claim. It made no political sovereignty demand. It was a defensive cultural preservation response to missionary aggression — oriented inward toward retention of classical tradition, not outward toward exclusion of others.<sup>\[14,15]</sup>

\
<br>

The 20th century Tamil political mobilisation — the Federal Party, the 50/50 demand, the language rights movement — was primarily constitutional and linguistic in character. Its intensification followed the Sinhala Only Act of 1956, not the other way around. Treating these two trajectories as symmetrical parallel ethnic mobilisations obscures an asymmetry that the chronological evidence plainly shows.<sup>\[15]</sup>

\
<br>

This is not a claim of Tamil innocence or Sinhalese guilt. It is a claim about the structure of the historical sequence. The constitutional Tamil political movement was responding to legislative exclusion through constitutional means — until those means were systematically closed by the 1972 removal of minority protections, at which point the conditions for armed resistance were created.<sup>\[15]</sup>

\
<br>

\
<br>

## IX. Toward a Single Identity: Tambapanni

The island needs a name that belongs to no ethnic group and predates all constructed identities. Tambapanni — the name used in Ashokan inscriptions in the 3rd century BC, by Ptolemy as Taprobane, by Roman traders, by Arab navigators as Serendib — is that name.<sup>\[7]</sup>

\
<br>

It carries no Buddhist, Hindu, Sinhalese, or Tamil ideological content. It is the island's identity in every external contemporary record from antiquity. It is geographic and ancient.

\
<br>

### What a Non-Projected Identity Looks Like

The island's actual historical identity, when the post-Westphalian and colonial lenses are removed, is not one of two separate peoples in ancient conflict. It is a civilisation that developed at the intersection of maritime trade routes, monsoon agriculture, and the meeting of Indo-Aryan and Dravidian linguistic and cultural traditions.<sup>\[5,20]</sup>

\
<br>

The hydraulic civilisation — the tank systems, the irrigation networks — was built by labour and engineering knowledge that crossed what we now call ethnic lines. The Buddhist monuments were patronised by kings of multiple linguistic backgrounds. The Palk Strait was not a border that divided two nations. It was the central highway of a connected civilisation.<sup>\[20]</sup>

\
<br>

### The Manufactured Rupture

The rupture — the sense of two ancient peoples in irreconcilable opposition — was not ancient. It was manufactured in the 19th century from European intellectual materials, hardened by British colonial administrative classification, and then detonated into political violence in the 20th century by actors on both sides who had internalised those colonial categories as primordial truth.<sup>\[15]</sup>

\
<br>

Understanding this does not resolve the political questions of the present. But it dissolves the false historical foundation on which those questions have been built — the claim that the ethnic conflict expresses an ancient and inevitable incompatibility between two peoples who have always been separate and opposed.

\
<br>

They were not separate. They were one civilisation with internal diversity — as all civilisations have been.

\
<br>

\
<br>

<p align="center"><em>The soil stained their hands copper.</em></p>

<p align="center"><em>That is all the Mahavamsa says about Tambapanni.</em></p>

<p align="center"><em>The rest is interpretation.</em></p>
